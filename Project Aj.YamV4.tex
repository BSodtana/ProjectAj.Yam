\begin{abstract}
Drug-drug interaction (DDI) prediction is an important task in computational pharmacology because interactions can alter drug absorption, metabolism, efficacy, and toxicity. However, many published evaluations rely on convenient data splits that allow overlap between training and test drugs, which can overestimate real-world generalization. This report presents a reproducible baseline for multi-typed DDI prediction on the DrugBank benchmark distributed through Therapeutics Data Commons under a realistic cold-drug protocol, where test drugs are unseen during training. The strongest validated configuration combines a Siamese graph attention network with 2048-bit ECFP4 fingerprints, logit adjustment with \(\tau = 0.5\), and deferred re-weighting in the final 30\% of training. Across four reported cold-drug folds, the model achieves mean accuracy of 0.6323, mean macro-F1 of 0.5019, mean macro PR-AUC of 0.5517, mean macro ROC-AUC of 0.9352, and mean Cohen's kappa of 0.5553. Among the reported folds, the top scores reach 0.6466 accuracy, 0.5266 macro-F1, 0.5942 macro PR-AUC, and 0.9422 macro ROC-AUC. A broader model comparison further shows that the \texttt{GAT + ECFP + LA + DRW} family achieves the strongest overall discrimination across reported runs, while calibration remains weaker than discrimination. These results establish a strong and transparent benchmark baseline for cold-drug DDI prediction and identify tail-class retrieval and probability calibration as important directions for future work.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} drug-drug interaction, graph attention network, ECFP, cold-drug split, logit adjustment, deferred re-weighting
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Background and Motivation}
Drug-drug interactions are a major concern in modern clinical practice because polypharmacy is common in chronic disease management, oncology, multimorbidity, and intensive care. Interaction events may increase toxicity, reduce therapeutic efficacy, or trigger clinically dangerous outcomes such as bleeding, arrhythmia, organ injury, or treatment failure. Structured resources such as DrugBank and benchmark frameworks such as Therapeutics Data Commons (TDC) have made DDI prediction increasingly accessible for machine learning research \cite{wishart2018drugbank, huang2021tdc}.

Despite this progress, DDI prediction remains difficult for three reasons. First, the label space is semantically diverse and often highly imbalanced. Second, many reported results depend on evaluation settings that allow the same drugs to appear in both training and test sets, which can inflate apparent generalization. Third, realistic deployment requires the model to reason about previously unseen compounds rather than memorizing known entities.

\section{Problem Statement}
Let \(d_a\) and \(d_b\) denote two drugs represented by SMILES strings. The task is to learn a classifier
\begin{equation}
f(d_a, d_b) \rightarrow y, \qquad y \in \{1,\dots,86\},
\end{equation}
where each class corresponds to a DrugBank interaction type. This work focuses on the cold-drug setting, in which test drugs are excluded from training, making the task substantially more realistic and more difficult than standard random splits.

\section{Research Objectives}
This project has four main objectives:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Build a reproducible end-to-end DDI prediction pipeline using RDKit, PyTorch, and PyTorch Geometric \cite{fey2019pyg}.
    \item Learn structure-aware representations of drugs using a Siamese graph attention network \cite{velickovic2018gat} augmented with fixed ECFP4 fingerprints \cite{rogers2010ecfp}.
    \item Improve long-tail performance through logit adjustment \cite{menon2021logit} and\\ deferred re-weighting inspired by imbalanced learning literature \cite{cao2019ldam}.
    \item Evaluate the resulting system under a realistic four-fold cold-drug protocol and quantify the contribution of fingerprint fusion.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}
The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A clearly documented cold-drug benchmark over 190{,}996 filtered DrugBank DDI rows.
    \item A strong reproducible baseline combining graph attention, ECFP fusion, logit adjustment, and deferred re-weighting.
    \item Four-fold reported cold-drug evaluation rather than a single split, providing a more stable view of model behavior after excluding the development fold from the final aggregate.
    \item An empirical analysis showing that ECFP improves discrimination while calibration remains a challenge.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Literature Review}
\section{DDI Benchmarks and Data Resources}
DrugBank is a widely used pharmacological resource that includes curated information about drug-drug interactions at scale \cite{wishart2018drugbank}. Therapeutics Data Commons standardizes access to DrugBank and other therapeutic learning tasks, enabling more reproducible experimentation \cite{huang2021tdc}. However, access to a benchmark alone does not solve the evaluation problem. If training and test partitions share the same drugs, models may exploit entity overlap rather than learn transferable chemical structure.

\section{Molecular Graphs and Graph Neural Networks}
Molecules are naturally represented as graphs, where atoms form nodes and bonds form edges. This makes graph neural networks a strong choice for molecular representation learning. Graph Attention Networks (GAT) use attention to adaptively weight neighboring nodes during message passing and have become a standard graph-learning architecture \cite{velickovic2018gat}. Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) are also relevant as a strong alternative and often serve as useful ablation baselines \cite{xu2019gin}.

In this project, molecular graphs are significant because they preserve the relational structure of each drug and allow the encoder to learn interaction-relevant chemical patterns directly from connectivity and atom-level features. This is particularly important in the cold-drug setting, where the model must generalize from molecular structure rather than drug identity.

\section{Fingerprint-Based Molecular Descriptors}
Although learned graph embeddings are powerful, classical cheminformatics descriptors remain competitive. Extended-Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP) encode local substructure information efficiently and have a long track record in molecular machine learning \cite{rogers2010ecfp}. In practice, graph-based and fingerprint-based representations are often complementary: graph encoders learn task-specific structural features, while fingerprints supply stable hand-engineered chemical patterns that may remain useful under limited supervision or long-tail label distributions.

\section{Related Work in DDI Prediction}
Recent DDI studies increasingly rely on graph-based modeling. Feng and Zhang proposed an attention-based graph neural network over molecular graphs for DDI prediction, showing that graph reasoning can capture chemically meaningful interaction signals \cite{feng2022gnnddi}. Han et al. introduced SmileGNN, combining SMILES-based and graph-based information for DDI prediction \cite{han2022smilegnn}. These studies support graph-centric approaches, but many published systems remain sensitive to how data are split and how labels are formulated.

\section{Long-Tail Learning and Calibration}
Class imbalance is a central challenge in multi-typed DDI prediction. Menon et al. proposed logit adjustment, which modifies logits using the log prior and can improve long-tail classification behavior \cite{menon2021logit}. Cao et al. introduced Label-Distribution-Aware Margin and deferred re-weighting (DRW), showing that weighting strategies are often more effective when applied later in training \cite{cao2019ldam}. These methods are well aligned with the present task, where rare interaction types are clinically meaningful but underrepresented.

A related issue is calibration. High discrimination does not guarantee reliable probabilities. In biomedical prediction, this distinction matters because a model may rank classes well while still producing overconfident or poorly calibrated outputs. The present work therefore reports expected calibration error, Brier score, and negative log-likelihood in addition to standard classification metrics.

\section{Research Gap}
The literature suggests a specific gap. Existing graph-based DDI methods support the use of molecular structure, but fewer studies jointly emphasize realistic cold-drug evaluation, a multi-class DrugBank interaction taxonomy, graph-plus-fingerprint fusion, and explicit long-tail optimization through logit adjustment and deferred re-weighting. This project addresses that gap by presenting a reproducible and deployment-oriented baseline rather than claiming a fundamentally novel architecture.

\chapter{Methodology}
\section{Pipeline Overview}
The overall pipeline contains five stages: dataset loading, split generation, graph and fingerprint construction, Siamese pair modeling, and evaluation. The implementation uses RDKit for molecular parsing, PyTorch for optimization, and PyTorch Geometric for graph neural network operators \cite{fey2019pyg}. Model selection is based on validation macro PR-AUC, which is particularly appropriate for imbalanced multi-class settings. Internally, labels are remapped to zero-based indices \(\{0,\dots,85\}\) so that the 86-way softmax head is aligned with the cross-entropy objective used in training and evaluation.

\section{Dataset Preparation and Cold-Drug Split Design}
The pipeline loads the DrugBank DDI benchmark through TDC and standardizes each row into three fields: \texttt{drug\_a\_smiles}, \texttt{drug\_b\_smiles}, and label \texttt{y}. The cold-drug splitter first canonicalizes each row into unordered form by lexicographically sorting the two SMILES strings, then groups rows by unordered pair key and removes ambiguous pair groups whose members carry more than one label. The reported benchmark uses split implementation v3 with deduplication policy \texttt{keep\_all}; for this DrugBank snapshot, every retained unordered pair has multiplicity 1, so the number of retained pair groups equals the number of retained rows.

The preprocessing summary is as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Raw rows: 191{,}808
    \item Raw unordered pair groups: 191{,}402
    \item Conflicting pair groups removed: 406
    \item Conflicting rows removed: 812
    \item Clean rows retained after filtering: 190{,}996
\end{itemize}

The selected configuration uses \(k=5\) folds, seed 42, protocol S1, guardrails of at least 5{,}000 test pairs and 45 test labels per fold, and a fold-selection objective of \texttt{selected\_fold}. First, a degree-aware greedy balancing procedure assigns each drug to exactly one fold. For outer fold \(f\), the validation fold is \((f+1)\bmod 5\) and the test fold is \(f\). A pair is assigned to training if both drugs belong to non-holdout folds, to validation if exactly one drug belongs to the validation fold and the other belongs to a training fold, and to test if exactly one drug belongs to the test fold and the other belongs to a training fold. All remaining pairs are excluded for that outer fold. As a result, validation and test pairs each contain exactly one drug unseen during training, unordered pair overlap across train/validation/test is zero by construction, and holdout--holdout pairs are intentionally discarded. Mean split statistics across folds and the strongest fold are shown in Table~\ref{tab:split_stats}.

\section{Molecular Graph and Fingerprint Features}
Each drug is converted from SMILES into a molecular graph using RDKit. Invalid or zero-atom molecules are skipped during featurization. Bonds are stored as directed edges in both directions; explicit self-loops are not added at featurization time, but each GAT layer adds self-loops internally. The node feature vector contains seven scalar dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item atomic number,
    \item degree,
    \item aromaticity flag,
    \item formal charge,
    \item hybridization code,
    \item ring membership flag,
    \item total hydrogen count.
\end{enumerate}

The edge feature vector contains five scalar dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item bond type,
    \item conjugation flag,
    \item aromaticity flag,
    \item ring membership flag,
    \item bond stereo code.
\end{enumerate}

Hybridization is encoded with the ordinal map \(\{\text{UNSPECIFIED}, \text{S}, \text{SP}, \text{SP2}, \text{SP3}, \text{SP3D}, \text{SP3D2}, \text{OTHER}\} \mapsto \{0,\dots,7\}\). Bond type uses \(\{\text{single}, \text{double}, \text{triple}, \text{aromatic}\} \mapsto \{1,2,3,4\}\), and bond stereo uses \(\{\text{none}, \text{any}, \text{Z}, \text{E}, \text{cis}, \text{trans}\} \mapsto \{0,\dots,5\}\).

In addition to graph features, the strongest baseline uses ECFP4 fingerprints with radius 2 and 2048 bits \cite{rogers2010ecfp}. The implementation uses RDKit Morgan fingerprints with binary output and \texttt{useChirality=False}. The fingerprint vector is projected by a learnable block \(\texttt{Linear}(2048,128) \rightarrow \texttt{LayerNorm} \rightarrow \texttt{ReLU} \rightarrow \texttt{Dropout}(0.2)\) before fusion with the graph embedding.

\section{Siamese Pair Model}
The model uses a Siamese architecture in which both drugs are encoded by the same graph attention backbone. The encoder contains three GAT layers with edge-aware attention and mean graph pooling. The first two layers use \(64\) hidden channels per head, \(4\) heads, concatenated multi-head outputs, ELU activation, and dropout 0.2. The final layer uses \(128\) output channels with \(4\) heads and \texttt{concat=False}, producing a \(128\)-dimensional pooled graph embedding \(\mathbf{g}\). Let \(\mathbf{g}_a\) and \(\mathbf{g}_b\) denote the graph embeddings for drugs \(a\) and \(b\), and let \(\mathbf{p}_a\) and \(\mathbf{p}_b\) denote the projected fingerprint vectors. Because the strongest baseline enables the ECFP pathway, each drug embedding is computed by
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_a = \phi([\mathbf{g}_a \| \mathbf{p}_a]), \qquad
\mathbf{h}_b = \phi([\mathbf{g}_b \| \mathbf{p}_b]),
\end{equation}
where \(\phi\) is a fusion block \(\texttt{Linear}(256,128) \rightarrow \texttt{ReLU} \rightarrow \texttt{Dropout}(0.2)\).

The pairwise representation is permutation-invariant in implementation. Define element-wise \(\mathbf{h}_{\min} = \min(\mathbf{h}_a,\mathbf{h}_b)\) and \(\mathbf{h}_{\max} = \max(\mathbf{h}_a,\mathbf{h}_b)\). The pair feature is then
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_{ab} =
[\mathbf{h}_{\min} \| \mathbf{h}_{\max} \| (\mathbf{h}_{\max} - \mathbf{h}_{\min}) \| \mathbf{h}_a \odot \mathbf{h}_b].
\end{equation}
With \(\mathbf{h}_a, \mathbf{h}_b \in \mathbb{R}^{128}\), the resulting pair feature dimension is 512. A classifier maps this representation through \(\texttt{Linear}(512,256) \rightarrow \texttt{ReLU} \rightarrow \texttt{Dropout}(0.2) \rightarrow \texttt{Linear}(256,86)\).

\section{Long-Tail Objective}
The model uses logit adjustment based on empirical train-split priors computed inside each fold with \(\epsilon = 10^{-12}\). Let \(\mathbf{s}\) denote the raw classifier logits and \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\) the class-prior vector. The adjusted logits are
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{s}} = \mathbf{s} + \tau \log \boldsymbol{\pi},
\end{equation}
where \(\tau = 0.5\) \cite{menon2021logit}. Cross-entropy is then applied to the adjusted logits, optionally with class weights:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathrm{CE}(\tilde{\mathbf{s}}, y; \mathbf{w}).
\end{equation}

During epochs 1--14, the model trains without class weighting. From epoch 15 onward, deferred re-weighting is activated and class weights are defined by
\begin{equation}
w_c = \mathrm{clip}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n_c + \epsilon}}, 0.25, 4.0 \right),
\end{equation}
where \(n_c\) is the number of training examples in class \(c\) and \(\epsilon = 10^{-12}\). In implementation, seen-class weights are normalized before clipping and are applied only after the DRW switch. At the same point, the learning rate is reduced from \(10^{-3}\) to \(2 \times 10^{-4}\). This schedule reflects the DRW intuition that shared feature learning should stabilize before minority-focused re-weighting is introduced \cite{cao2019ldam}.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
Deterministic seeding is applied to \texttt{random}, NumPy, PyTorch, CUDA, and DataLoader workers. Validation is computed after every epoch, the best checkpoint is selected by validation macro PR-AUC, and split artifacts, train priors, class counts, class weights, and feature-cache metadata are persisted under the output directory. The software stack is pinned in \texttt{requirements.txt}, including PyTorch 2.4.1, PyTorch Geometric 2.5.3, RDKit 2023.9.6, NumPy 1.26.4, pandas 2.2.2, and scikit-learn 1.5.1.

Evaluation includes accuracy, macro-F1, micro-F1, Cohen's kappa, macro ROC-AUC, macro PR-AUC, expected calibration error, Brier score, objective loss, and negative log-likelihood. Accuracy, micro-F1, and kappa use the argmax of the adjusted softmax distribution. Macro-F1 is computed over labels present in the evaluation targets only. Macro ROC-AUC and macro PR-AUC are reported as one-vs-rest averages over classes that contain both positive and negative examples in the evaluation split; macro PR-AUC uses \texttt{average\_precision\_score}. ECE uses 15 equal-width bins on max-softmax confidence, and the multiclass Brier score is the mean squared error between the predicted probability vector and the one-hot target vector. Because some folds lack positives or negatives for particular classes, the evaluator also reports the number of classes excluded from one-vs-rest scoring. Tail macro PR-AUC is computed over the bottom \(\lceil 0.2 \times 86 \rceil = 18\) classes ranked by training support, including zero-count classes.

\section{Training Dynamics and DRW Behavior}
A representative strong run reaches its best validation checkpoint at epoch 18. At that point, validation accuracy is 0.6390, macro-F1 is 0.5246, macro PR-AUC is 0.5678, and macro ROC-AUC is 0.9417. Performance improves rapidly in the early epochs and continues to improve after the DRW phase begins, supporting the decision to delay class re-weighting until later in training.

This behavior is consistent with prior imbalanced-learning findings that aggressive weighting can destabilize early optimization \cite{cao2019ldam}. In this pipeline, DRW functions as a late-stage correction mechanism that improves minority-sensitive performance without dominating initial representation learning. Figure~\ref{fig:drw_transition} provides a visual explanation of the deferred re-weighting mechanism. Before epoch 15, the loss remains class-neutral, allowing the model to learn a stable shared representation. After the DRW switch, class weighting shifts more strongly toward tail classes, increasing their contribution to the optimization objective. This transition coincides with a visible split between the objective loss and plain negative log-likelihood and is followed by improved validation macro PR-AUC.

The gain in macro PR-AUC is particularly important because it indicates improved performance across the imbalanced label distribution. At the same time, expected calibration error worsens, showing that stronger discrimination does not automatically yield more reliable probabilities. This trade-off is a meaningful practical finding rather than a weakness to be hidden. In biomedical settings, this distinction matters because a highly confident but poorly calibrated model may be less trustworthy than a slightly weaker but better calibrated alternative. Future work should therefore evaluate post-hoc calibration strategies such as temperature scaling, Platt scaling, or isotonic regression to improve probability reliability without sacrificing ranking quality.

\section{Tail-Class Performance}
Rare interaction types remain difficult. Mean tail macro PR-AUC is 0.2107, substantially below overall macro PR-AUC. This gap shows that long-tail recognition remains a bottleneck even after logit adjustment and deferred re-weighting. However, the strongest reported fold reaches 0.2837 tail macro PR-AUC, suggesting that the current baseline can recover useful minority-class structure when class coverage is favorable. The remaining weakness likely reflects both low data support and greater structural heterogeneity among rare classes, especially under a cold-drug partition where the model cannot rely on previously seen compounds. Some tail classes may therefore be difficult not only because they are underrepresented, but also because they contain chemically diverse or semantically overlapping interaction patterns.

\section{Discussion}
The results support four main conclusions. First, the cold-drug setting is feasible but substantially challenging, making performance at this level meaningful. Second, molecular graphs are an appropriate representation because they allow the model to generalize from transferable chemical structure rather than drug identity. Third, fingerprint fusion adds complementary information beyond learned graph embeddings. Fourth, long-tail optimization improves classification behavior, but tail retrieval and probability calibration remain open problems.

The implementation is also reproducibility-oriented. It includes deterministic seeding, split persistence, graph and fingerprint caching, diagnostic summaries, and tests for core components such as splitting and logit adjustment. The repository also contains explanation modules, but predictive performance under the validated cold-drug baseline is the central contribution of the present report. Interpretability is therefore better described as an extensible capability of the system rather than a fully established headline result in this version.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
This report presented a reproducible baseline for multi-typed drug-drug interaction prediction under a realistic cold-drug protocol. The strongest validated configuration combines a Siamese graph attention architecture, ECFP4 fingerprint fusion, logit adjustment, and deferred re-weighting. Across four reported folds, the system achieves mean accuracy of 0.6323, mean macro-F1 of 0.5019, mean macro PR-AUC of 0.5517, and mean macro ROC-AUC of 0.9352, with the strongest reported fold reaching 0.6466 accuracy and 0.5942 macro PR-AUC.

The main conclusion is that learned graph representations alone are not sufficient to capture all interaction-relevant chemical structure in this setting. Performance improves materially when graph embeddings are fused with fixed chemical fingerprints and optimized using a long-tail-aware training schedule. At the same time, the remaining gap in tail-class retrieval and the increase in calibration error show that ranking quality and probability reliability should be treated as distinct objectives.

Future work should focus on four areas. First, tail-class performance should be improved through stronger long-tail objectives, re-sampling strategies, or hierarchical label modeling. Second, calibration should be tightened using post-hoc methods such as temperature scaling. Third, multimodal representations could extend the current molecular-only formulation by incorporating targets, pathways, or heterogeneous biomedical knowledge graphs. Fourth, explanation modules should be evaluated on the strongest final baseline so that interpretability claims rest on the same experimental foundation as the predictive results.

In summary, the project now provides a strong research-grade foundation for cold-drug DDI prediction: realistic evaluation, transparent methodology, reproducible experimentation, and a validated baseline that is well suited for further publication-oriented development.
